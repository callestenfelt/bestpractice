Page type · Article

Article Page

A single piece of editorial content, usually long-form. The reader is here to read; everything else on the page either supports that or gets in the way.

Before you start

Page purpose & audience 3 items
Decide the one thing this article should accomplish before opening the editor.
strategy concept content Nielsen Norman Group 2024-04-08

Articles that try to inform, persuade and convert in equal measure usually do none of them well. Pick the primary user task — read for understanding, follow a how-to, evaluate a position — and let it shape every other decision: title length, lead structure, where (if anywhere) a CTA sits.

If you cannot finish the sentence "after reading this, the user should be able to ___" in fewer than 12 words, the brief isn't done yet.

Source. Nielsen Norman Group · How users read on the web View source ↗
Map the article to a single dominant search intent — informational, navigational, transactional, or commercial.
seo content strategy web.dev 2025-11-02

Search engines and increasingly LLM-based answer engines route queries to pages by best-matching intent. A page that mixes intents (e.g. a how-to with three paragraphs of brand history before the steps) competes for nothing well.

Write a one-line intent statement next to the working title. If the body drifts, either fix the body or fork the article.

Source. web.dev · Content and SEO View source ↗
Aim for a reading level one or two grades below your audience's expected literacy ceiling.
content ux W3C / WCAG 2.2 · SC 3.1.5 2023-09-21

WCAG SC 3.1.5 (Reading Level, AAA) suggests that when text requires reading ability more advanced than lower-secondary, a simpler version or supplemental content should be available. In practice: short sentences, common words, glossed jargon.

Hemingway, Datayze, or built-in word processor tools all approximate this. Don't tune for the score — tune for the reader.

Source. W3C · WCAG 2.2 Understanding 3.1.5 View source ↗
Editorial brief & angle 3 items
New. Lead with the angle in the brief, not the topic. "Why X is overrated" beats "Notes on X".
content concept A List Apart 2026-05-06

A topic ("typography on mobile") is the room; an angle ("variable fonts make the case for fewer breakpoints") is what you do in it. The brief should commit to the angle before a single paragraph is drafted.

Editors test this with the "and so?" question. If the angle survives three of them, ship the brief.

Source. A List Apart · The angle test View source ↗
Length follows argument, not the other way around. Don't pad to hit a target word count.
content seo strategy Nielsen Norman Group 2024-02-19

The "1,500 words for SEO" rule of thumb is a misreading of correlation studies. Search engines reward articles that satisfy intent fully; an intent satisfied in 400 words doesn't get more satisfied by 1,100 more words.

Inverse rule: if your draft is short, ask whether you've over-trimmed structure. If it's long, ask whether you've under-edited argument.

Source. Nielsen Norman Group · Long vs short articles as content strategy View source ↗
Pin tone of voice to two or three adjectives in the brief, with one anti-adjective for each.
content design strategy GOV.UK Design System 2025-07-30

"Confident but not arrogant; clear but not patronising; warm but not cute." The anti-adjective is the work — without it, "confident" drifts to "shouty" without anyone noticing.

If two writers in the team disagree on whether a paragraph fits, the brief's anti-adjectives are usually the missing referee.

Source. GOV.UK · Content design: planning, writing and managing content View source ↗

URL structure 4 items
Use lowercase kebab-case. Hyphens separate words; underscores do not.
seo frontend Google Search Central 2024-08-14

Google's URL structure guide explicitly recommends hyphens over underscores for word boundaries. Underscores are treated as word-joiners; type_inference is one token, type-inference is two.

Capitals work but introduce case-sensitivity surprises on some servers and in user expectations. Lowercase everything.

Source. Google Search Central · URL structure best practices View source ↗
Keep dates out of the URL unless the article is genuinely time-bound (e.g. an annual report).
seo strategy maintenance Moz 2024-01-15

A URL like /2021/05/typography-on-mobile/ ages the article by definition. When you update it in 2026, you either lie via the URL or break every existing link.

Flat slugs (/typography-on-mobile/) are durable. Use datePublished and dateModified in structured data for the date facts.

Source. Moz · URL best practices View source ↗
Keep slugs under ~60 characters; truncate the working title, don't extend it.
seo content Google Search Central 2024-08-14

Long URLs get truncated in shared previews and look untrustworthy. Strip stop words ("a", "the", "and", "of") from the slug; the title element can keep them.

If you can drop a word from the slug and a reader who pasted only the URL still understands the topic, drop it.

Source. Google Search Central · URL structure best practices View source ↗
When you change a slug, ship a 301 from the old URL. Always. No exceptions.
seo backend maintenance MDN 2025-03-04

A 301 transfers PageRank, signals permanence to crawlers, and preserves backlinks. 302s don't — they say "we'll be back". Use 302 only for genuine temporary redirects (A/B tests, geo-routing).

Keep a redirect map under version control. Stale redirects pile up; review once a year.

RewriteEngine On
Redirect 301 /old-slug /new-slug
Source. MDN · HTTP redirections View source ↗
Eyebrow / kicker 2 items
Use the eyebrow for section or topic, not as a second headline.
content design ux Nielsen Norman Group 2024-10-08

The eyebrow ("Opinion", "Tutorial", "Long read", "Performance") tells the reader what kind of article they're about to read. It's a frame, not content. Two to four words. ALL CAPS or letterspaced small caps both work.

Semantically the eyebrow is part of the heading group. Use a <p> with role context, or wrap title and eyebrow in <hgroup> (now well-supported in WHATWG HTML).

Source. Nielsen Norman Group · Eyebrow text in editorial design View source ↗
If the eyebrow only repeats the section name from the breadcrumb, drop it; visual hierarchy beats redundancy.
frontend ux The A11y Project 2024-12-11

Screen reader users get the breadcrumb and the eyebrow back-to-back; two pieces of the same information feels like a stutter. Audit content so eyebrow earns its row.

Source. The A11y Project · Headings posts View source ↗
Page title & H1 5 items
WCAG 2.4.6 requires headings and labels to describe topic or purpose.
ux frontend W3C / WCAG 2.2 · SC 2.4.6 2023-09-21

SC 2.4.6 (AA) is the rule against clever, opaque headings. Screen reader users navigate by heading; cute titles that hide the topic ("Down the rabbit hole") force a full read just to know what the page is.

Article H1: state the subject. Reserve cleverness for the lead.

Source. W3C · WCAG 2.2 SC 2.4.6 Headings and Labels View source ↗
Match the H1 to the <title> element unless SEO demands a longer title.
seo content frontend Nielsen Norman Group 2024-09-12

The on-page H1 and the document <title> are usually the same. When they diverge, the title carries the search-engine load — site name suffix, secondary keyword — and the H1 carries the editorial load.

On mobile. Browser tabs truncate at ~25 characters. Front-load the most distinctive word.

On desktop. Titles up to ~60 characters render in full in Google's SERP. Suffix style: Title — Site name.

Source. Nielsen Norman Group · Page titles View source ↗
New. One H1 per page. Google has tolerated multiple H1s since 2019; that doesn't make it a good idea.
frontend seo web.dev 2026-05-04

Assistive tech still uses the document outline to convey "what page is this". Multiple H1s flatten it. The HTML5 outline algorithm that promised <section>-scoped H1s never shipped in browsers or screen readers — write H1–H6 manually.

Source. web.dev · Document outline and headings View source ↗
Aim for ~50–70 characters in the H1. Long enough to be specific, short enough to scan.
content design Nielsen Norman Group 2024-09-12

Headings that exceed two display lines stop being scannable. Use text-wrap: balance; to keep the break point graceful at narrow widths.

h1 { text-wrap: balance; }
Source. Nielsen Norman Group · Microcontent: a few words go a long way View source ↗
Front-load distinctive words. The reader's eye and the SERP both lose attention left-to-right.
ux content seo Nielsen Norman Group 2024-09-12

Eye-tracking work going back to the early F-pattern studies shows that the first two words of a heading do disproportionate work. "An introduction to variable fonts" buries the lede; "Variable fonts: an introduction" earns the click.

Source. Nielsen Norman Group · F-shaped pattern of reading View source ↗
Lead / standfirst 3 items
The lead's job is to make the reader continue. Don't summarize the article — promise it.
content ux Nielsen Norman Group 2024-06-30

Standfirsts that summarize give the reader permission to leave. Standfirsts that tease commit to a payoff the body has to deliver. Bias toward tease, then make the body keep the promise.

Source. Nielsen Norman Group · Inverted pyramid: writing for comprehension View source ↗
One or two sentences, around 25–40 words. Set in a larger size than body copy.
content design The Economist style guide 2023-11-04

A larger size (1.2×–1.4× body) and slightly tighter line height visually distinguish the lead from the body. Resist the urge to colour it; weight and size carry enough.

Source. The Economist · Style guide: introductions View source ↗
Don't mark the lead up as an H2. It's display-styled body text — use a <p> with a class.
frontend seo MDN 2024-12-01

A heading announces a section. The lead doesn't start a section, it sits inside the article's implicit "intro". Marking it H2 splits the outline and confuses screen reader navigation.

If you want a sub-headline above the lead, use <hgroup>:

<hgroup>
  <h1>Variable fonts: an introduction</h1>
  <p class="lead">One file, every weight, every width…</p>
</hgroup>
Source. MDN · <hgroup>: the heading group element View source ↗
Byline & dateline 4 items
Attribute to a named human. Generic "Editorial team" bylines hurt trust signals and EEAT.
content strategy seo Google Search Quality Guidelines 2024-11-20

EEAT (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness) weighs author identity. Link the byline to a profile page with credentials. AI-assisted articles should still carry the human editor's byline; disclose the assistance in the body.

Source. Google · Search Quality Rater Guidelines View source ↗
Show both Published and Updated when they differ — and explain in the body what changed.
content seo Nielsen Norman Group 2025-01-22

Stealth-republishing an old article with a fresh date is dishonest and Google penalises the pattern. Show both dates; if the update was substantive (>20% rewrite), add a brief changelog line at the bottom.

Source. Nielsen Norman Group · Updated content and trust View source ↗
Wrap dates in <time datetime="…"> with ISO 8601 in the attribute.
frontend seo MDN 2025-02-18

The datetime attribute carries the unambiguous machine value; the element's text can be the prettified human form. Crawlers, calendars, and assistive tech all read the attribute.

<time datetime="2026-05-15T09:30:00+02:00">15 May 2026</time>
Source. MDN · <time>: the time element View source ↗
Reading-time estimates help, but use them honestly: 220–250 wpm for technical reading, not 300.
ux content Medium engineering blog 2023-08-15

The classic Medium formula assumed 265 wpm at average reading difficulty. Technical articles slow down; round up. Display as "~7 min read", not "6 min 42 s" — false precision suggests false confidence.

Source. Medium · Read time and Medium View source ↗

Body

Body typography & readability 5 items
Cap measure at 60–75 characters per line. Wider needs leading; narrower hurts rhythm.
design frontend Butterick's Practical Typography 2024-03-19

Use the ch unit and let the browser do the work:

.prose { max-width: 66ch; }

Beyond ~75 characters the eye loses the next line; below ~45 it scans more than it reads.

Source. Butterick · Practical Typography: line length View source ↗
Base font size 16–18px. Anything smaller fails AA-level readability for many users.
design frontend ux W3C / WCAG 2.2 · SC 1.4.4 2023-09-21

Use rem for body sizing so the user's browser zoom preference takes effect. WCAG SC 1.4.4 requires text to resize to 200% without loss of content; that fails if the layout is built in px with fixed containers.

Source. W3C · WCAG 2.2 SC 1.4.4 Resize Text View source ↗
Set body line-height between 1.5 and 1.7 (unitless). Tight leading is for headings.
design frontend W3C / WCAG 2.2 · SC 1.4.12 2023-09-21

SC 1.4.12 (Text Spacing) requires the page to survive user-applied line-height of 1.5× body size. Building to that floor is the simplest path to compliance.

Source. W3C · WCAG 2.2 SC 1.4.12 Text Spacing View source ↗
New. text-wrap: pretty is now Baseline newly available — use it on body prose for better orphans.
frontend design caniuse 2026-05-08

Browsers do a final-line balance pass to avoid single-word last lines. Cheap, no JS, no harm if unsupported.

.prose p { text-wrap: pretty; }
.prose h1, .prose h2, .prose h3 { text-wrap: balance; }
Source. caniuse · CSS text-wrap: pretty View source ↗
Enable hyphenation only with a language attribute on <html>, and only with a justified or narrow column.
frontend design MDN 2024-07-09

Hyphenation rules are language-specific. Without lang="en" the browser falls back to nothing or to the wrong dictionary, producing ugly breaks. At ragged-right widths above ~60ch hyphenation usually loses; turn it on for narrow columns only.

Source. MDN · CSS hyphens View source ↗
In-article media 4 items
Always set width and height attributes on <img>. Eliminates CLS.
frontend measurement web.dev · Core Web Vitals 2025-09-04

The attributes give the browser an aspect ratio to reserve space before bytes arrive. Setting height: auto in CSS preserves responsive scaling. CLS often drops by an order of magnitude after this single change.

<img src="hero.jpg" width="1600" height="900" alt="…" />
Source. web.dev · Optimize Cumulative Layout Shift View source ↗
Use loading="lazy" for below-the-fold images. Never on the hero — it delays LCP.
frontend web.dev 2025-04-22

Pair loading="lazy" with decoding="async" to free up the main thread. For the LCP candidate image, do the opposite: fetchpriority="high", loading="eager", preconnect to the image origin.

Source. web.dev · Use lazy-loading to improve loading speed View source ↗
Alt text describes function, not appearance. A photo of a developer at a laptop is "a developer pair-programming", not "a man at a computer".
content frontend ux The A11y Project 2024-10-14

The test: read the article aloud with the image replaced by its alt text. Does it make sense? Decorative images that add nothing to the prose take alt="" (not missing — empty), removing them from the accessibility tree.

Source. The A11y Project · Write alternative text View source ↗
Captioned media goes in <figure>/<figcaption>. Captions are not alt text; they complement it.
frontend content MDN 2024-05-30

Alt text describes what the image is; the caption adds editorial context, attribution, or interpretation. They can repeat, but should not duplicate verbatim.

Source. MDN · <figure>: the figure with optional caption element View source ↗
Inline links 3 items
Link the words that describe the destination. Never "click here", "read more", "this".
content ux frontend W3C / WCAG 2.2 · SC 2.4.4 2023-09-21

Screen readers can list every link on the page in isolation. "Click here" eleven times is unusable. The link text alone should answer "where does this go?"

Source. W3C · WCAG 2.2 SC 2.4.4 Link Purpose View source ↗
Underline inline links. Colour alone is not a sufficient signal (WCAG 1.4.1).
design frontend ux W3C / WCAG 2.2 · SC 1.4.1 2023-09-21

Use text-underline-offset and text-decoration-thickness to dial in an underline that respects descenders without looking heavy.

a {
  text-decoration-thickness: 1px;
  text-underline-offset: 2px;
}
Source. W3C · WCAG 2.2 SC 1.4.1 Use of Color View source ↗
Don't force target="_blank" for editorial links. Users own that decision; respect them.
ux content Nielsen Norman Group 2024-03-31

The middle-click and cmd-click conventions exist; they're discoverable; users use them. Forcing a new tab breaks the back button, surprises keyboard users, and bypasses the user's preference. Reserve target="_blank" for true tool-style escape hatches (PDF previews, embedded forms).

When you do use it, pair with rel="noopener" to close the window-reference vector. Modern browsers default this; setting it explicitly is harmless.

Source. Nielsen Norman Group · Opening links in new browser windows View source ↗
Pull quotes & callouts 3 items
Pull quotes belong in <aside>, not in <blockquote>. They're an editorial flourish, not a citation.
frontend content MDN 2024-08-19

A pull quote duplicates body content for emphasis. A blockquote cites external content. Confusing the two creates duplicate text for screen readers; <aside> with aria-hidden="true" on the pull quote is the cleanest pattern.

Source. MDN · <aside>: the aside element View source ↗
Use <blockquote cite="…"> for actual quotations; pair with a <cite> element for attribution.
frontend content seo MDN 2024-08-19 
<blockquote cite="https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/">
  <p>Provide ways to help users navigate…</p>
  <footer>— <cite>WCAG 2.2</cite></footer>
</blockquote>

The cite attribute carries a URL machines can parse; the <cite> element carries the human-readable source name.

Source. MDN · <blockquote>: the block quotation element View source ↗
Editorial callouts ("Tip", "Note", "Warning") don't need ARIA role="alert" — they're not interactive.
frontend ux The A11y Project 2025-02-04

ARIA role="alert" is for transient, dynamic messages screen readers should interrupt for. Static editorial callouts are body content with a visual treatment; mark them up as <aside> or <div> with a clear textual label ("Note:").

Source. The A11y Project · ARIA View source ↗

End of page

Related content 3 items
Editor-curated "related" beats algorithmic. Recency and topical-affinity feeds bury the best content.
content strategy Nielsen Norman Group 2024-11-12

"Most recent" is a recency filter, not a relevance filter. For a 1,200-article archive, automated relatedness produces predictable misses. The cheapest fix is two manually-picked links per article in the CMS.

Source. Nielsen Norman Group · Related items in editorial design View source ↗
Show 3–5 related items. Beyond five and the user disengages; below three feels token.
ux design Baymard Institute 2024-06-04

Baymard's editorial-site testing puts the engagement sweet spot at four. Below three the section looks unfinished; above five it becomes a list, which gets skimmed and skipped.

Source. Baymard Institute · Article page related content View source ↗
Each related item needs context: a one-sentence "why this is here", not a bare title.
content ux A List Apart 2024-05-21

Bare titles ask the reader to evaluate "is this worth my time?" with too little signal. A line of context is editorial work that pays for itself in click-through.

Source. A List Apart · Editing related content View source ↗
Social sharing 3 items
Prefer navigator.share() on mobile; fall back to a copy-link button.
frontend ux MDN · Web Share API 2025-08-12

The Web Share API hands off to the user's native share sheet — every app they use, ordered by their habits. It's better than any custom button row.

Feature-detect:

if ('share' in navigator) {
  // show share button
} else {
  // show copy-link button
}
Source. MDN · Navigator.share() View source ↗
A wall of platform-specific share buttons usually hurts performance more than it helps reach.
strategy ux measurement Smashing Magazine 2024-04-26

Most third-party share widgets ship tracking, fonts, and 100kB+ of JS. Measure click-through before optimising visual prominence; you'll likely find a copy-link button outperforms a six-button strip.

Source. Smashing Magazine · The hidden cost of share buttons View source ↗
Confirm "link copied" with a toast or in-place text change. Silent success feels broken.
ux frontend design Nielsen Norman Group 2024-10-29

For screen reader users, fire an aria-live="polite" region with "Link copied" so the action announces. Visual confirmation alone fails non-sighted users.

Source. Nielsen Norman Group · System status visibility View source ↗

Behind the scenes

Meta description 3 items
Aim for ~150–160 characters. Google routinely truncates beyond ~158, and longer values get rewritten.
seo content Google Search Central 2024-08-14

Google selects the snippet at query time. If your description is too long, irrelevant to the query, or absent, Google generates one from the page body. Write it for humans; the bot will use it if it matches intent.

On mobile. Snippets render shorter — front-load the first 60 characters with the answer.

On desktop. You get the full ~158 characters in most layouts.

Source. Google Search Central · Create good meta descriptions View source ↗
Don't stuff keywords into the meta description. It's a snippet, not a ranking factor.
seo maintenance Moz 2024-01-15

Meta descriptions haven't been a ranking signal since 2009. Their only job is to win the click. Write them like the back of a book.

Source. Moz · Meta description View source ↗
Every page gets a unique description. Duplicates trigger "duplicate meta description" warnings in Search Console.
seo content maintenance Google Search Console docs 2025-06-30

If you can't write a unique one, omit it and let Google generate. An auto-generated snippet from real body content beats a templated one that lies.

Source. Google · Search Console: meta descriptions View source ↗
Structured data (Article schema) 4 items
Required Article fields for Google: headline, image, datePublished, author.
seo frontend backend schema.org 2024-12-19

Google's Article rich result requires these four; everything else (publisher, dateModified, description) is recommended. Validate before deploying with Google's Rich Results Test.

<script type="application/ld+json">
{
  "@context": "https://schema.org",
  "@type": "Article",
  "headline": "Variable fonts: an introduction",
  "image": "https://example.com/hero.jpg",
  "datePublished": "2026-05-15",
  "author": { "@type": "Person", "name": "J. Riley" }
}
</script>
Source. schema.org · Article View source ↗
Use JSON-LD, not microdata or RDFa. Google has preferred it since 2017.
seo frontend Google Search Central 2024-08-14

JSON-LD lives in a single <script type="application/ld+json"> tag, separate from your markup. It survives template refactors. Microdata couples structured data to specific tags and tends to drift out of sync.

Source. Google · Introduction to structured data View source ↗
Pick the most specific type: NewsArticle, BlogPosting, TechArticle — not generic Article.
seo content schema.org 2024-12-19

Subtypes inherit Article's fields and add their own. NewsArticle unlocks the Top Stories carousel; TechArticle hints at documentation context for AI summarisation. Picking the right one is a small lift with disproportionate effect.

Source. schema.org · NewsArticle, BlogPosting, TechArticle View source ↗
New. Author must be a Person with at minimum name; add url to a stable author page.
seo content Google Search Central 2026-05-10

Google's 2024 update to the Article guidelines hardened the author requirement: it should be a Person (not Organization) with a name, ideally a stable URL the crawler can revisit. EEAT signals depend on this.

Source. Google · Article structured data View source ↗
Open Graph & Twitter Cards 3 items
Four required OG fields: og:title, og:type, og:url, og:image.
seo frontend content Open Graph protocol 2023-12-01

For articles, og:type is article. Add og:description for the preview snippet. Most LLM-based answer engines (Perplexity, ChatGPT search, Claude) read OG as the canonical title/description; treat it as first-class metadata.

Source. Open Graph protocol · The Open Graph protocol View source ↗
og:image at 1200×630px, under 5MB, in PNG or JPEG. Text on the image should be ≥40px.
design frontend Open Graph protocol 2023-12-01

1200×630 is the safe aspect ratio across LinkedIn, Slack, Facebook, X, Discord. Some platforms crop to square; keep the focal subject inside the centre 630×630 region.

Auto-generate per-article OG images server-side. The investment pays back across years of shares.

Source. Open Graph protocol · Image best practices View source ↗
For X / Twitter, set twitter:card to summary_large_image. Don't duplicate fields OG already covers.
frontend seo X Developer Platform 2024-04-02

X falls back to the corresponding OG tag for any Twitter field you omit (image, title, description). The only Twitter-specific tags worth setting are twitter:card and twitter:site.

Source. X · Twitter Cards documentation View source ↗

Site-wide considerations

Layered onto every page
Colour contrast 2 items
Body text contrast ≥ 4.5:1 (AA), ≥ 7:1 (AAA). Large text ≥ 3:1.
design frontend ux W3C / WCAG 2.2 · SC 1.4.3 2023-09-21

WCAG 2.2 still uses the relative-luminance formula; WCAG 3 (draft) will replace it with APCA. For now design to the established floor and validate with a tool that reports both, so you're not surprised when WCAG 3 ships.

Source. W3C · WCAG 2.2 SC 1.4.3 Contrast (Minimum) View source ↗
Non-text contrast ≥ 3:1. Catches: form borders, focus rings, chart strokes, icon-only buttons.
design frontend W3C / WCAG 2.2 · SC 1.4.11 2023-09-21

This rule retroactively makes the popular pale-grey-on-white input border illegal. If a control's edge is the only thing distinguishing it from the page, it needs the 3:1 floor.

Source. W3C · WCAG 2.2 SC 1.4.11 Non-text Contrast View source ↗
Keyboard navigation 2 items
Tab order follows visual reading order. Don't use positive tabindex; rearrange the DOM instead.
ux frontend W3C / WCAG 2.2 · SC 2.4.3 2023-09-21

Positive tabindex overrides natural order and tends to drift out of sync with future edits. tabindex="0" and tabindex="-1" stay useful.

Source. W3C · WCAG 2.2 SC 2.4.3 Focus Order View source ↗
Use :focus-visible, not :focus. Mouse clicks shouldn't paint the focus ring.
design frontend MDN 2024-08-19

Baseline-supported since 2022. The browser decides when focus is "keyboard-style" and paints the ring only then. Pair with :focus for older browsers if needed.

button:focus-visible {
  outline: 2px solid var(--blue-9);
  outline-offset: 2px;
}
Source. MDN · :focus-visible View source ↗
